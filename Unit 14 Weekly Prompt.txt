Question 1: Colormaps are used extensively with scientific data. How are these applied when visualizing measurable phenomenon like temperatures or rainfall? Reference your textbook or the internet. Cite examples that you find interesting.

Colormaps are used to connect numbers to colors so people can understand measurable data faster. Instead of looking at a long list of rainfall totals or temperatures, I can look at a map or graph and quickly see where the values are higher or lower. In Python Crash Course, Eric Matthes explains that a colormap is a gradient of colors used to emphasize patterns in data. The book gives an example where lower values can be lighter and higher values can be darker, which helps the points vary smoothly across the chart.

For temperature, a weather map might use cooler colors like blue or purple for lower temperatures and warmer colors like yellow, orange, or red for higher temperatures. That makes sense because the color choice matches how people already think about hot and cold. For rainfall, a map might use lighter colors for small amounts of rain and darker blues, greens, or purples for heavier rain. Matplotlib’s documentation says sequential colormaps are good for ordered data, which fits temperature and rainfall because the numbers move from low to high.

One example I find interesting is NOAA precipitation maps. NOAA uses color maps for precipitation frequency estimates, but they also point out that the maps are visual aids, meaning the colors help interpretation but should not replace the actual numbers. That stood out to me because color makes data easier to read, but it can also oversimplify things if I do not check the scale. So, a good colormap should not just look nice. It should make the data clearer and more accurate.
